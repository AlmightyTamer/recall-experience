Chapter 1 · The problem

Every 65 seconds, someone develops Alzheimer's.

When the system fails, families absorb the shock alone. Dementia isn't only a medical diagnosis — it's a years-long coordination problem spanning medications, cognition, safety, identity, and grief. The infrastructure meant to help largely doesn't exist at home.

Where care breaks down

Families absorb five problems at once.

Each one is a full-time job. Together, they're a years-long emergency with no playbook — and almost no tools built for the home.

Medications

Missed & doubled doses.

A daily, high-stakes guessing game with no reliable second set of eyes.

Cognition

Invisible decline.

The slide happens between appointments, where no one is measuring.

Safety

Wandering & sundowning.

Crises arrive without warning — usually when no caregiver is in the room.

Identity

"Who are you?"

Losing names and faces erodes dignity faster than any single symptom.

Grief

Caring while mourning.

Families grieve a living person, alone, for years — and burn out.

The gap

No home infrastructure.

The support meant to help mostly doesn't exist where care actually happens.
